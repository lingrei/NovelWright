# Pacing Techniques

> Craft reference for controlling narrative pace at every structural level.

## Core Concept

Pacing is the writer's control over the reader's experience of time. A novel contains two timelines: story time (how long events take in the fictional world) and reading time (how long the reader spends on those events). The ratio between them is pace. A week can pass in a sentence. A single second can take three pages. The writer decides.

---

## Scene vs. Summary

The most fundamental pacing decision is whether to render a moment as a **scene** (real-time, dramatized, with dialogue and action) or as **summary** (compressed narration covering time efficiently).

| Mode | Story Time : Reading Time | When to Use |
|------|---------------------------|-------------|
| **Scene** | 1:1 or slower | Emotional turning points, confrontations, revelations, key decisions. Anything the reader should *experience*. |
| **Summary** | Compressed (days/weeks/months in a paragraph) | Transitions, routine periods, necessary-but-undramatic information. Anything the reader should *know*. |
| **Half-scene** | Selectively expanded | Open in summary, drop into a scene for the key moment, then pull back out. Useful for covering ground while preserving one vivid beat. |

**The rule:** If a moment matters emotionally, it gets a scene. If it matters only informationally, it gets summary. The most common pacing error is dramatizing the wrong moments -- writing full scenes for logistical beats while summarizing emotional ones.

## Sentence-Level Pacing

### Short Sentences

- Create urgency, speed, impact
- Mimic adrenaline and heightened perception
- Best used in bursts, not sustained (sustained short sentences read like a telegram)

> He ran. The door was locked. He hit it with his shoulder. It held. He hit it again.

### Long Sentences

- Create immersion, contemplation, sensory richness
- Pull the reader into a continuous perceptual stream
- Best used when the character or narrator is processing, observing, or remembering

> She sat in the car for a long time after the engine died, listening to the ticking of the metal as it cooled, watching the porch light of the house she'd grown up in flicker with the particular electrical hesitation that meant the wiring still hadn't been fixed, that nothing inside had changed, that walking through that door would return her to a version of herself she had spent a decade outrunning.

### The Shift

The most powerful pacing move at the sentence level is the transition from one mode to the other. A long passage followed by a short sentence creates impact. A rapid sequence followed by a long, slow sentence creates relief or reflection.

> [Long] The afternoon had been ordinary in every respect -- the kind of Tuesday that existed only to fill the space between Monday and Wednesday, unremarkable in its weather, its errands, its steady accumulation of small tasks completed and forgotten.
>
> [Short] Then the phone rang.

## Paragraph-Level Pacing

### Paragraph Length

- **Dense paragraphs** (8-12 lines) slow the reader. They signal importance, complexity, or immersion. The reader settles in.
- **Short paragraphs** (1-3 lines) speed the reader. They create urgency or emphasis.
- **Single-line paragraphs** are dramatic pauses. Use sparingly -- they lose force through repetition.

### White Space

The blank line between paragraphs is a beat of silence. In high-tension scenes, increasing the frequency of paragraph breaks (more white space) creates the rhythm of rapid breathing. In calm scenes, longer unbroken paragraphs create the rhythm of steady, deep breathing.

## Chapter-Level Pacing

### Chapter Hooks

The final line of a chapter controls whether the reader turns the page or puts the book down.

| Hook Type | Mechanism | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Question** | Introduce an unanswered question | "That was the last time I saw the house with all its windows intact." |
| **Reversal** | Flip an expectation | "She opened the door. The apartment was empty. All of it -- the furniture, the photographs, the evidence that anyone had ever lived there at all." |
| **Arrival** | Introduce a new element | "The phone on the counter buzzed. She didn't recognize the number, but she recognized the area code." |
| **Withholding** | Cut away before resolution | End mid-conversation, mid-confrontation, mid-realization. |

### Chapter Length Variation

Uniform chapter length creates monotony. Vary chapter length to match content:

- Short chapters (2-4 pages) during high-action sequences create a sense of rapid forward motion.
- Long chapters (15-25 pages) during complex emotional sequences allow immersion.
- A very short chapter (1 page or less) after several long ones creates emphasis through structural surprise.

## Advanced Techniques

### Time Dilation

Expand a single moment beyond its natural duration. Useful for moments of crisis, beauty, or revelation.

> The glass left her hand. She watched it turn once in the air -- slowly, impossibly slowly, catching the overhead light on its rim so that for a fraction of a second it wore a halo -- and she had time to notice the way the water inside curved against the glass, the way it refused to separate from the vessel that held it, loyal until the very end, and then it hit the floor and was nothing.

The glass falls for perhaps one second. The prose gives it fifty words. The reader experiences the character's dilated perception.

### Time Compression

Collapse a long period into a single image or sentence. Useful for showing the weight of duration without the tedium of living through it.

> She waited. The coffee went cold. She made another cup. That one went cold too.

Four sentences cover what might be hours. The repetition communicates duration through pattern rather than clock time.

### Interruption Pacing

Cut a scene before its natural resolution and jump to a different thread. The unresolved tension carries across the gap, making the reader want to return. Alternate between two or more storylines at moments of peak tension.

### Slow-In, Fast-Out

Enter scenes slowly (establish setting, mood, spatial relationships) and exit them quickly (cut as soon as the emotional peak lands). Lingering after the climactic moment deflates it. The reader should leave the scene still feeling the impact.

---

## Diagnostic Questions

When revising for pace, ask:

1. **Am I dramatizing the right moments?** Mark every scene. Does each one earn its real-time treatment?
2. **Where does the reader's attention flag?** Those passages are either too slow for their content or too long for their importance.
3. **Where does the reader feel rushed?** Those passages need expansion -- the emotional content requires more reading time than the prose currently provides.
4. **Is there variation?** A novel at one consistent speed is a novel at no speed. Pace is only perceptible through contrast.
