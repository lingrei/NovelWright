# Physical Gesture Library

> Craft reference for physical behaviors organized by emotional state.
> Use as a starting point, not a lookup table. The best gesture for any moment is the one that belongs to that specific character in that specific situation.

---

## Nervousness / Anxiety

1. Picking at a cuticle or hangnail until it bleeds
2. Turning a ring around a finger, faster as tension builds
3. Pressing a thumbnail into the pad of the opposite thumb
4. Adjusting clothing that doesn't need adjusting -- straightening a collar, pulling down a hem
5. Tapping a foot in an irregular rhythm (not steady -- arrhythmic, as though the body can't decide)
6. Holding a glass without drinking from it, condensation running over the knuckles
7. Beginning a sentence, stopping, starting a different one

## Anger (Controlled)

8. Setting an object down with excessive precision -- aligning a pen parallel to a notebook edge
9. Speaking more quietly than normal, each word given exactly the same weight
10. Standing very still while others move around them
11. Folding arms not across the chest (defensive) but behind the back (restrained)
12. Smiling with the mouth only -- the eyes remain fixed
13. Rearranging things on a surface: straightening papers, capping a pen, squaring a phone

## Anger (Uncontrolled)

14. Jabbing a finger toward someone but not quite touching them -- the last inch of restraint
15. Pushing back from a table hard enough that the chair legs scrape
16. Gripping the edge of a counter or table until the tendons stand out in the forearm
17. A sudden, sharp exhalation through the nose -- not a sigh, closer to a bull's warning breath
18. Pacing a short distance and turning -- three steps, turn, three steps, turn

## Grief / Sadness

19. Pressing the heel of the hand against the eye socket -- not wiping tears, just pressure
20. Going very still, as though movement might cause something to break
21. Performing a small, routine task with exaggerated focus (folding a napkin, tying a shoe) -- the mind seeking anything to hold onto
22. Starting to speak and producing only a cracked exhalation
23. Holding an object that belonged to the absent person without looking at it

## Affection / Tenderness

24. Tucking a strand of hair behind someone's ear when it doesn't need tucking
25. Adjusting the other person's collar, scarf, or lapel -- grooming as intimacy
26. Walking slightly closer than necessary, shoulders almost touching
27. Passing food from their plate to another's without being asked
28. Looking at someone while that person looks at something else -- the unwitnessed gaze

## Discomfort / Wanting to Leave

29. Shifting weight from one foot to the other
30. Checking a watch or phone, then checking again ten seconds later without having registered the time
31. Angling the body toward the nearest exit while keeping the face pointed at the conversation
32. Picking up keys, bag, or coat and holding them -- not yet leaving, but pre-loading the departure
33. Agreeing too quickly and too often ("Right. Yep. Absolutely. Right.")

## Deception / Concealment

34. Maintaining eye contact slightly too long -- overcompensating for the instinct to look away
35. Touching the neck or throat while speaking (self-soothing, subconscious vulnerability)
36. Offering excessive detail about an irrelevant part of the story
37. Becoming very still in the hands while the voice remains casual
38. Laughing a half-beat too late

## Confidence / Authority

39. Taking up space -- spreading papers across a table, draping an arm over the back of a chair
40. Holding a pause after being asked a question, letting the silence work
41. Leaning back rather than forward -- making others come to them
42. Cutting food, pouring a drink, or performing a manual task without looking at it -- attention stays on the other person

---

## Usage Notes

- **Specificity over category.** "She was nervous" can be replaced by any item in the nervousness list. But the *right* item depends on who she is. A surgeon picks at cuticles differently than a teenager does. Find the gesture that belongs to this character.
- **Gesture change = character change.** If a character who always controls their anger (items 8-13) suddenly displays uncontrolled anger (items 14-18), the reader registers the shift as seismic. Track which gestures belong to a character's baseline and which represent departure.
- **Less is more.** One precisely chosen gesture per emotional beat. Stacking three gestures in the same paragraph dilutes all of them.
- **Avoid cliches.** "Clenched fists," "gritted teeth," "rolled eyes," and "raised eyebrows" have been used so often they've become invisible. When a gesture appears in every novel, it stops being a gesture and becomes punctuation. Find the version that is specific to your scene.
