# Dialogue as Character

> Calibration reference for dialogue that reveals identity.

## Principle

Dialogue is not a delivery mechanism for plot information. Every line a character speaks is an act of self-revelation. The reader should be able to identify the speaker without any dialogue tag -- not because of a catchphrase, but because the sentence structure, word choice, and rhetorical strategy are unique to that person's mind.

Key disciplines:

- **Vocabulary is biography.** A former soldier does not say "I'm scared." She says "This position is untenable." Word choice reveals education, profession, region, era, and self-image.
- **Rhythm is temperament.** Anxious characters fragment. Controlled characters complete their sentences. Arrogant characters monologue. Listen to how long each character is willing to let a sentence run.
- **What they don't say matters.** Evasion, deflection, subject-changing, and silence are all dialogue. The shape of what a character avoids tells the reader what they are protecting.
- **No two characters should argue the same way.** One retreats to logic. One escalates to volume. One goes quiet. Conflict style is character.

## Example Passage

> "The numbers don't support it. I've run them twice." Helen squared her papers against the table edge. "If we extend the lease, we're underwater by March."
>
> Across the table, Jim tilted his chair back until it creaked. "Helen. It's a bar. People have been drinking in that building since before your spreadsheet was born."
>
> "That's not an argument."
>
> "Sure it is. You just don't have a column for it." He let the chair drop forward. "Ask anyone on Broad Street what that corner means. Go ahead. Bring your clipboard."
>
> She removed her glasses and set them on the papers. "Sentiment doesn't service debt, Jim."
>
> "And debt doesn't keep a neighborhood, Helen."

### What to notice

- **Helen** speaks in complete, structured assertions. Her physical gestures are precise (squaring papers, removing glasses). She reaches for quantitative language ("numbers," "underwater," "service debt").
- **Jim** speaks in fragments and rhetorical challenges. His body language is loose (tilting, dropping the chair). He reaches for concrete, communal images ("people drinking," "Broad Street," "that corner").
- Neither character is wrong. The dialogue reveals not just their positions but the architecture of how they think.
- Zero dialogue tags after the opening. The reader never loses track of who is speaking.
