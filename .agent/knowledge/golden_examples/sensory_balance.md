# Sensory Balance

> Calibration reference for multi-channel sensory writing.

## Principle

The human body receives the world through five channels simultaneously. Fiction that defaults to vision alone produces flat, cinematic prose -- the reader watches but never inhabits. Effective sensory writing weaves at least three channels (visual, tactile, auditory, olfactory, gustatory) into a single passage so the reader's nervous system fires alongside the character's.

Key disciplines:

- **Lead with the unexpected channel.** If a scene begs for visual description (a sunset, a grand hall), open with sound or smell instead. The visual will land harder when it arrives second.
- **Anchor abstract emotion in physical sensation.** Grief is not described; it is the thickness at the back of the throat, the wool-heavy air in a closed room.
- **Rotate channels within a paragraph.** No three consecutive sentences should rely on the same sense.

## Example Passage

> The kitchen still smelled of last night's garlic, sharp and faintly sweet beneath the cold. Margaret set her palm flat on the countertop and felt the chill of the granite travel up through her wrist. Outside, a crow called once -- a single raw syllable that broke against the fog and disappeared. She could see her breath. The tap dripped behind her, each drop landing in the steel basin with a sound like a fingernail tapping glass. She picked up the mug her daughter had left. The ceramic was room temperature now, the tea inside dark and still, and when she brought it close, she caught the ghost of chamomile, faint as a word someone decided not to say.

### What to notice

- **Olfactory** opens the passage (garlic).
- **Tactile** follows immediately (cold granite through the wrist).
- **Auditory** arrives twice (crow call, dripping tap), each with a distinct texture.
- **Visual** is used sparingly and precisely (breath, dark tea).
- **Olfactory** closes the passage (chamomile), bookending the paragraph.
- The emotional weight (absence, a daughter who has left) is never stated -- it lives entirely inside the sensory details.
