# Inner Monologue

> Calibration reference for internal voice and body-mind dissonance.

## Principle

The most revealing moments in fiction occur in the gap between what a character thinks and what they do. Inner monologue is not a window into the character's "real" feelings -- characters lie to themselves as fluently as they lie to others. The power of internal voice is in the friction: the reader sees what the character tells themselves, watches what the character actually does, and assembles the truth in the space between.

Key disciplines:

- **The character's internal narration is unreliable.** They rationalize, minimize, deflect. A character thinking "I'm fine" while gripping the steering wheel until their knuckles whiten is telling two stories at once. The reader believes the knuckles.
- **Internal voice has its own diction.** Thought does not sound like narration. It is clipped, repetitive, associative, full of half-finished sentences. Formal internal monologue reads false.
- **Use the body as counterpoint.** The body does not lie the way the mind does. When the internal monologue says one thing and the hands, stomach, or breathing say another, the reader trusts the body.
- **Irony lives in the gap.** The reader knows more than the character precisely because they can see both the thought and the action. This dramatic irony is one of fiction's most potent tools.

## Example Passage

> It didn't bother her. That was the first thing Elise decided, standing in the hallway with the phone still warm against her ear. It didn't bother her because she'd expected it -- she'd known since November, really, known the way you know a bridge has ice before you feel the tires slide. So it didn't bother her.
>
> She set the phone face-down on the hall table and walked to the kitchen. Took a glass from the cabinet. Filled it from the tap. Drank half of it standing at the sink, looking out at the yard where the sprinkler was hitting the fence in a steady, idiot rhythm. She finished the water. Rinsed the glass. Set it in the rack.
>
> Then she picked up the phone and read the text again.
>
> The sprinkler ticked against the fence. She read it a third time.

### What to notice

- **Repetition of "it didn't bother her"** is the internal voice working overtime to construct a narrative of composure. The triple assertion undermines itself -- if it truly didn't bother her, she wouldn't need to say it three times.
- **The body tells a different story.** She executes a precise, mechanical sequence of actions (glass, fill, drink, rinse, rack). This is the behavior of someone holding themselves together through routine.
- **The return to the phone** demolishes her internal claim. She can't leave it alone.
- **The sprinkler** operates as an external metronome -- steady, mindless, continuing whether or not she falls apart. Its indifference is the emotional weight of the scene.
- At no point does the prose say "she was devastated." The reader arrives there alone, which makes the feeling land harder.
