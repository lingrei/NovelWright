# Show, Don't Tell

> Calibration reference for concrete physical behavior over abstract emotional statement.

## Principle

"She was angry." This sentence asks the reader to accept an emotional label. "She set her fork down with exaggerated care, lining it up parallel to the knife, and said nothing for the rest of the meal." This sentence asks the reader to watch a person and draw their own conclusion. The second version is more work to write and more work to read, and it is immeasurably more powerful, because the reader who diagnoses the anger themselves believes in it more than the reader who is told.

Key disciplines:

- **Replace emotional adjectives with physical behavior.** Every abstract state (nervous, happy, grieving, furious) has a physical signature. Find it. The hands, the posture, the breathing, the small involuntary actions -- these are the vocabulary of showing.
- **Specificity is the engine.** "She was sad" could describe any person in any story. "She pressed her thumbnail into her palm until the crescent turned white" belongs to one person in one moment. Specificity creates the sensation of witnessing a real human being.
- **Trust the reader.** The instinct to tell often comes from fear that the reader won't "get it." They will. Readers are sophisticated interpreters of human behavior -- they do it every day in life. Give them the evidence and step back.
- **Telling is not always wrong.** Summary narration, time-skips, and transitional passages often require telling. The rule is: the more emotionally important the moment, the more it demands showing. Reserve telling for what connects the shown moments, not for the moments themselves.

## Example Passage

> #### Told
>
> Richard was devastated by the news. He felt like his world had collapsed. He was overwhelmed with grief and didn't know what to do with himself. The loss was unbearable.
>
> #### Shown
>
> Richard took his coat off the hook by the door, put it on, and walked to the end of the driveway. He stood there for a while. Then he walked back inside, hung the coat on the hook, and sat down in the chair by the phone. After a minute he stood up, took the coat again, put it on, and walked to the end of the driveway.
>
> He did this four times before his neighbor, watching from across the street, came over and guided him back inside.

### What to notice

- **The told version** uses four sentences to label the same emotion four slightly different ways. The reader receives information but no experience. There is nothing to see.
- **The shown version** never uses the words "grief," "devastated," or "loss." Instead, the reader watches a man whose internal compass has broken -- he can't complete the simple decision of whether to stay or go. The repetition of the coat-driveway loop communicates a malfunction deeper than sadness.
- **The neighbor** arriving does critical work: it externalizes the severity. Richard's behavior is alarming enough that someone intervenes. The reader sees the grief through two sets of eyes.
- **Physical specificity** grounds it: the hook by the door, the chair by the phone, the driveway. These are not generic props -- they are the architecture of a particular life, now disrupted.
